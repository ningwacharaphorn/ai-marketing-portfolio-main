### 💡 Strategic Takeaways from Chris Savage:

Chris’ core insight is that the internet has fundamentally changed; platforms are now designed to keep users in-feed, making "clicks" an unreliable primary signal for growth. Success now depends on influence and memory—creating content that drives branded search and long-term recall rather than immediate traffic.

1. **Stop optimizing for clicks—focus on influence**
   - The click is no longer the primary driver of growth as users stay within native platforms.
   - *Strategic Mandate:* Target the result of influence: being remembered, learned from, and searched for later.

2. **Measure what’s remembered, not just what’s easy**
   - Marketing teams often over-reward measurable metrics (clicks/traffic) while undercounting the content that actually builds demand.
   - *Strategic Mandate:* Look for signals like increased branded search and people mentioning your content weeks after consumption.

3. **Value "Zero-Click" content**
   - Content should stand on its own without requiring a "link in bio" or a click to finish the thought to provide real value.
   - *Strategic Mandate:* Deliver the core idea and utility directly within the platform to maximize its memorable impact.

### 🎯 Summary of Chris Savage’s Approach (Influence-First Content Strategy):
Based on the post’s message:
- **Influence over attribution** wins in a platform-locked internet.
- **Branded search and recall** become the core success metrics for modern teams.
- **Standalone utility** matters more than driving immediate external traffic.
- **Optimizing for memory** ensures that when customers are ready to buy, they come looking for you specifically.

[Everyone says: “optimize for clicks.”

But what if the click is no longer the thing that actually drives growth?

The internet changed.

People don’t leave platforms like they used to. Feeds, inboxes, search, AI everything is designed to keep you there.

But most marketing teams are still operating like it’s 2015.

Clicks.
Traffic.
Attribution.

And because of that, we’re systematically undercounting what’s working…and over-rewarding what’s easiest to measure.

I was talking to Amanda Natividad on Talking Too Loud, and the conversation completely reframed how I think about the internet today. 

The shift is simple, but uncomfortable:

The click isn’t the primary signal anymore. It’s increasingly the result of influence.

And influence means:

Are people learning from you?
Do they remember you?
Will they search for you later?

Can you relate to this?

You put something out into the world…it doesn’t drive a ton of clicks.

But then:
Branded search goes up
People mention it weeks later
Someone says, “I’ve been seeing your stuff everywhere”

You can’t draw a straight line.

And that’s exactly why most teams ignore it.

That’s the trap:
If you only optimize for clicks…you’ll miss the content that’s actually creating demand.

So what should you do instead?

Make things that stand on their own.
No “click to finish the thought.”
No “link in bio for the real value.”

Just:
Give the idea.
Make it useful.
Make it memorable.

Because when this works…people don’t click. They come looking for you.
Most teams are still optimizing for what’s measurable.

The best teams are optimizing for what’s remembered.

And those two things are diverging faster than most people realize.]
